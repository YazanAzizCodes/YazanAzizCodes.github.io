Most inventory optimization frameworks were designed for commercial environments: elastic demand, substitutable parts, and suppliers you can swap in weeks.

Defense programs break every one of those assumptions.

## What Makes It Different

**Demand is operationally driven.** Parts requirements aren't shaped by market signals — they're driven by flight schedules, deployment configurations, and maintenance forecasts derived from sensor telemetry. Miss a forecast by 10%, and aircraft sit on the ground.

**Lead times are measured in years.** For diminished manufacturing sources (DMS) parts, a supply gap might not close for 18–36 months. Algorithms have to plan that far out.

**The cost of a stockout isn't financial — it's readiness.** That asymmetry fundamentally changes the optimization objective.

## A Better Framing

Instead of minimizing holding cost against a stockout probability, we optimize for *readiness coverage* — ensuring projected demand across all participants is met, while surfacing both surplus (waste) and deficit (risk) positions explicitly.

The key inputs:
- Flight-hour forecasts by participant
- Historical consumption rates by configuration
- Current inventory positions and pipeline quantities
- Supplier lead times and production capacity

The output isn't just a reorder quantity. It's a ranked list of parts to act on, with explicit surplus/deficit classifications that drive procurement decisions and redistribution across the global supply network.

## What I've Learned

The algorithm is rarely the hard part. The hard part is data quality — getting consistent, trustworthy inventory snapshots from systems that weren't designed to talk to each other. Garbage in, garbage out applies twice as hard when the stakes are this high.

Clean data pipelines and disciplined data governance are worth more than any sophisticated model built on top of unreliable inputs.
